Title: How to Write Content That Gets Cited by LLMs
(AKA how to become the source the internet learns from)
Source: @scalerrs.co

1. Why LLM Citations Matter
ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity are becoming default search tools for users. It doesn't stop there...

LLMs generate 1B+ answers daily. Visibility is shifting from SERP to AI.
SGE, ChatGPT, and AI overviews cite brands with structured, trusted answers.
CTR from organic search is dropping. AI is the new top-of-funnel.
Content cited by AI drives brand recall and earns trust fast.


2. Focus on Specific, Evergreen Questions
Broad advice rarely gets cited. LLMs look for precise answers to real queries.

Go for long-tail, intent-heavy queries like "saas pricing page examples"
Prioritize "how to," "why," and "best way to" formats
Use Reddit, forums, PAA, and support logs to mine real questions
Keep topics evergreen with regular updates


3. Answer the Question Right Away
Don't bury the lede. LLMs favor content with a clear, concise answer up top.

Open with a clear 2–3 line answer
Expand with examples, exceptions, or step-by-step walkthroughs
Use TL;DR or summary boxes at the top
Avoid intros that stall. LLMs skim fast.


4. Use Semantic Structure and Schema
LLMs parse structure and clarity before style.

Use proper heading hierarchy: H1 → H2 → H3
Apply FAQPage, HowTo, and Article schema
Add tables, bullets, charts, and alt-texted visuals
Present data-rich comparisons (features, pricing tiers, use cases)
Clearly state who each option is for ("best for agencies," etc.)


5. Add Unique Insight & Internal Know-How
LLMs love novel inputs.

Include original frameworks, stats, or quotes
Share internal workflows, decision-making logic, or behind-the-scenes processes
Use screenshots or product breakdowns to illustrate your approach
Present data-rich comparisons (features, pricing tiers, use cases)
Teach something not found in the top 5 search results


6. Show Real-World Sentiment & Social Proof
LLMs also look for content grounded in actual user experience.

Summarize Reddit, forum, or comment section sentiment
Embed top-voted quotes with attribution
Include testimonials, UGC, or curated audience feedback
Present data-rich comparisons (features, pricing tiers, use cases)
Reference survey results or support insights when relevant
Make it feel lived-in, not corporate.


7. Mention Your Brand Consistently
LLMs index brands that show up with context.

Add your brand name near frameworks, quotes, visuals
Use 1–2 sentence bios under author names on content
Create brand entity profiles across Wikipedia, Crunchbase, LinkedIn
Cross-reference content across your blog, third-party blogs, forums, etc.


8. Get Cited by Authority Sources
You need secondhand credibility too.

Publish guest posts or expert quotes on high-trust domains
Get mentioned in newsletters, roundups, podcasts, or tools
Pitch your frameworks to authors writing LLM-indexed content
Link from indexed content to your source pages


9. Build Community Reputation Signals
LLMs pull from Reddit, Quora, and forums when official sources lack depth.

Monitor and engage in relevant subreddits or niche forums
Reference forum sentiment to reflect real-world buyer discussions
Embed or quote high-karma comments with clear context and takeaways
Use community consensus to support or contrast your recommendations


10. Keep It Updated & Fresh
LLMs prefer current, trustworthy info.

Add updated timestamps to high-performing pages
Refresh examples, update outdated stats, remove broken links
Use tools like Profound or Ahrefs to track LLM citations
Repurpose high-performing posts into other LLM-friendly formats like Video


11. Play Long-Term Games
Remember, you won't become an AI source overnight. But if you consistently Teach → Show → Solve, you'll start seeing your content show up in:

AI summaries
SGE answers
Cited sources in tools like Perplexity


12. LLM Content Checklist (Save This)
If you want your content to be picked up by LLMs:

Start with a real question and answer it up top
Use structured headers, lists, and schema
Embed unique insights and real-world data
Mention your brand contextually and consistently
Earn mentions from trusted sources
Reflect community sentiment and use cases
Keep content updated and optimized for parsing. ]

[Most people think Google ranks your page based on content, keywords, links...etc
But this isn't the full picture.

Google also care about how users engage with your pages.

Over the years working with SaaS brands, i'll share what I’ve seen again and again:

Great content still underperforms.
Because users land, skim, get lost… and bounce.

Here’s how that shows up in Google’s eyes:

→ Low time on page
→ Weak scroll depth
→ People bouncing back to search (pogo-sticking)
→ Zero CTA interaction

The problem isn’t the content. It’s the UX.

In SaaS, especially, here’s where things go wrong:

- Pricing is buried in footers or awkward menus
- Blog layouts read like essays, not guides
- Pages talk features, not problems users are solving

If you want stronger user signals, fix these first:

✅ Front-load the value → Tell users exactly what they’ll get in the first 2 lines.

✅ Structure for skimming → Use H2s that signal outcomes, not just topics.

✅ Time your CTAs → Place them where your product solves a problem.

✅ Add micro-interactions → Use tables, accordions, or sticky menus to drive clicks.

✅ Surface key info early → Show pricing or features where curiosity naturally peaks.

✅ Cut filler → Remove intros or copy that don’t help users scroll, click, or decide.

The truth is, if your SEO stops at keyword targeting, you’re doing half the job.

UX should never be 'just' an option, a good-to-have. It's a must,
It’s what turns rankings into revenue.
