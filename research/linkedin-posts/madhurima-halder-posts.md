## Post 1 — [April 2026]
[“Can we just use AI and push out content faster?”

I get why this sounds smart. 

More content feels like more surface area in search. 

However, a recent 16-month study by Search Engine Land tested this idea at scale. 

They launched 20 fresh websites and published 2,000 AI-written articles with no edits.

The early results looked promising.

Pages got indexed quickly. Some even started ranking for a good number of keywords. 

And traffic showed up sooner than expected.

Then things changed...

Around the three-month mark, visibility dropped hard. 

The pages were still indexed, but they stopped showing up in key positions.

Nothing meaningful came back even after a year.

What went wrong was not hard to see.

The content had no real voice or depth that came from experience. It did not give Google or readers a reason to trust it.

I keep saying this: publishing more is always doable. Standing out isn't. 

It requires experience and effort.

AI makes it easy to produce content. But it does not make that content worth reading or ranking.

Search still leans on signals that take time to build. That includes trust, expertise, and a clear point of view.

When those pieces are missing, speed works against you.

You end up scaling something that was never strong to begin with.

The teams that win use AI very differently. They use it to support thinking, not replace it.

They still bring real insights to the table, shape content around what they know and still invest in authority.

That brings me to E-E-A-T.

It stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness and Trustworthiness. 

Google (to date) still uses these signals to decide what deserves visibility. 

And none of this comes from publishing faster.

AI can support research, speed up drafts, and help structure ideas. It cannot replace experience or build authority on its own.

That is why content that looks fine on day one often fades later.

## Post 2 — [March 2026]
[If I had to start SEO from scratch today, I would not begin with a content calendar.

That step creates activity, but it does not create direction.

I would begin by understanding (in-depth) how search works. I mean how Google reads a query, how it evaluates pages, and why certain results stay on top for long periods.

That layer of clarity shapes every decision that follows.

The next step would be to focus on one narrow problem space. This is not a broad topic or a traffic goal but a specific situation where someone is actively looking for an answer.

That focus gives you a clean entry point into search.

I would then spend time studying what already ranks. This goes beyond scanning headlines. 

It involves looking at structure, depth, and how well each result solves the query.

Weaknesses are usually easy to spot once you slow down and read properly.

Content comes after that.

At this stage, I would create a small set of pages that are tightly connected. 

Each page would serve a clear purpose in the buyer's journey and support the others.

Distribution would play a role from the beginning. 

Getting the content in front of the right audience helps you see how it performs before search traffic kicks in.

Search Console becomes the main source of truth here. It shows how queries connect to your pages and where traction starts to build.

Over time, patterns begin to show. You see which topics expand, which pages hold attention, and where you can go deeper.

That is when scaling starts to make sense.

SEO works best when it is built on understanding, not output.

A lot of teams produce content consistently. Fewer teams take the time to understand 'why' something works.

That gap is where most organic strategies break.

If you were rebuilding your SEO today, where would you start?]

## Post 3 — [January 2026]
[If you want better results from SEO, start by stopping this one thing👇

Stop creating content “for a keyword” without validating what the searcher actually wants.

Most SEO plateaus come from a simple mismatch. 

You publish a guide when Google is rewarding product pages. 

You publish a comparison page when the SERP is dominated by “what is” explainers. 

Google’s systems are built to surface content that gives searchers a satisfying experience, not content that exists mainly to rank. 

You can do everything “right” on-page and still lose because the format is wrong for the query (intent mismatch).

You waste months scaling pages that were never eligible to win because the SERP is telling you what it wants, and you ignored it.

You end up with near-duplicate “keyword variant” pages that confuse Google and dilute authority, which is exactly the type of pattern Google has warned about with doorway-style pages.

What to do instead:
1. Run a 3-minute SERP reality check before writing
Open the top results and call the intent in plain English: “learn,” “compare,” “buy,” “fix,” “template,” “tool.” Search intent is literally the reason behind the query, and aligning to it is the baseline for performance.

2. Match the dominant format, then out-execute it
If the winners are listicles, don’t publish a guide. If the winners are landing pages, don’t publish a glossary. When your result matches intent, it’s far more likely to earn strong engagement signals and ranking stability.

3. Consolidate instead of multiplying pages
If you have five posts chasing tiny variations of the same query, you’re paying interest on your own mess. Merge, redirect, and build one page that actually satisfies the job-to-be-done, because “more pages” is not a strategy. Google’s long-standing stance is that pages created primarily for search engines degrade user experience. 

Steal this ⤵️
If you removed the keyword from the doc, would the page still be obviously useful to a real human? If not, it’s probably “search-first” content wearing a people-first costume. 

My team and I can run a quick exercise for you. 

If you tell me your niche and one target keyword, I’ll do the SERP intent call and tell you the exact page type that should win (and what to stop publishing immediately).]
